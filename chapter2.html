Chapter II

The Collector's Echo

✦ ✦ ✦

The rain in Veleslav tasted like ash. Lily traced the condensation on her bedroom window, the distorted streetlights blurring into ghostly halos. She had been in the town for barely a week, yet an insidious unease had already settled beneath her skin. A feeling of being… watched.

It wasn't simply fear. Beneath the apprehension, something else stirred. A quiet, persistent curiosity that refused to be ignored.

A sharp rap at the door startled her.

Old Man Hemlock, the landlord, had mentioned leaving a spare key "just in case." Lily hadn't asked for it. She hadn't even seen him since signing the lease.

The key itself was wrong.

Heavy. Antique brass, tarnished with age. Too deliberate to be practical. It didn't fit the front door. Or the windows. Or anything that should have mattered.

Frustration flickered through her. She turned the key over in her fingers, ready to dismiss it, when something caught her attention — a subtle irregularity in the wall behind a faded tapestry.

A panel.

Small. Almost imperceptible. Had it always been there? She stepped closer, her pulse quickening. The fabric felt colder than it should have as she pulled it aside. The outline was clearer now. Not imagined.

Placed.

The key slid in with a soft, decisive click. The panel opened.

Inside, there was no jewelry. No money. No valuables. Only photographs. Faded. Sepia-toned. Faces she didn't recognize, dressed in outdated clothing, their expressions stiff… but wrong in a way she couldn't immediately define.

Not empty. Not neutral. Haunted.

Lily exhaled slowly and picked one up. Then another. And another. At first, it was nothing. Just people. Strangers frozen in time.

Then — something shifted.

A presence in the background of one of the images. Barely visible. A distortion more than a figure. She frowned, leaning closer. Another photograph. The same thing. Not the same place. Not the same people. But the same… shape.

Too still. Too deliberate.

Her fingers tightened around the edges of the photograph as a quiet, creeping realization began to form. She grabbed another image, her movements sharper now, more urgent. Flipping through them faster, searching — not for faces anymore, but for him.

There. Again. And again. Always just outside the moment. Never the subject. Always watching. The angle of the shoulders. The tilt of the head. The absence of blur where there should have been motion.

Zane.

The name surfaced without permission. Without doubt. Lily swallowed hard, her breath uneven now. She turned each photograph over, searching for something — anything — that would make sense of it. Dates. Names. Clues. Most were blank.

Until one wasn't.

A portrait of a young woman with hollow, lifeless eyes. The paper was thinner, more worn than the others. And on the back, in elegant, deliberate handwriting, a single word:

Taken.

Lily froze. The word didn't feel descriptive. It felt final.

A sudden gust of wind rattled the windowpanes. She turned. Across the street, beneath the flickering glow of a lamppost, he stood.

Zane.

Not moving. Not hiding. Just… there. Rain slid down his coat, darkening the fabric, but he didn't react to it. He wasn't looking at the building — not directly. His posture was relaxed, almost casual. But something about it was wrong. Too still. Too aware. As if he had been there long before she noticed. And as if he had been waiting for her to.

Her breath caught. Slowly, as though drawn by something she couldn't resist, her gaze locked onto his. The distance between them didn't matter. She felt it. That same precision. That same quiet, invasive awareness.

Not curiosity. Recognition.

Zane didn't smile. Didn't move closer. Didn't signal. He simply held her gaze. A beat too long. Then, almost imperceptibly, his head tilted. Not a greeting. Not a warning.

A question.

Lily's pulse surged. Fear rose — but something else pushed against it, sharper, hotter. A defiance she hadn't expected. Who are you? The question burned behind her lips, but no sound came. Neither of them looked away. And for a moment — brief, impossible — it felt like the entire city had gone silent around them.

Then — he stepped back. And the darkness took him. Just like that. Gone.

Lily remained frozen by the window, her reflection faintly staring back at her in the glass. This wasn't random. It wasn't coincidence. It was placement. And somewhere, beneath the unease clawing at her chest, a single, undeniable truth settled in:

She hadn't just found something hidden. She had been meant to find it.
