Chapter IV

The Line

✦ ✦ ✦

The silence stretched longer than it should have. It wasn't empty. It was waiting.

Lily felt it settle around her — not like pressure, but like expectation. As if something had already leaned forward, listening for what she would say next.

She should have stood up. She knew that. But she didn't.

The café hadn't changed. But something in her had.

Zane watched her, his expression unreadable. He hadn't moved since offering her the choice. He was giving her a way out. A clear, simple path: turn, walk through the door, forget everything. And she wasn't taking it.

Not because she felt brave. Not because she felt chosen. Because the not-knowing felt worse than the knowing might.

It was a quiet, stubborn defiance. A trait she'd always carried but never named: she hated not understanding things. It was the reason she studied folklore, the reason she dug into myths about tricksters and deceivers. Ignorance was a poison. And she'd rather drink the truth, even if it burned.

She didn't stand. She didn't walk away. Instead, she asked a question.

"Who didn't leave when they should have?"

Zane's eyes narrowed, just slightly. A fractional shift. Not surprise. Reassessment.

"That," he said, his voice a low murmur, "wasn't the answer I expected."

It was the first crack. The first moment he hadn't predicted her perfectly.

He leaned back, but the distance between them didn't increase. It changed in quality. He was no longer just an observer. He was engaged.

"Alright," he said, almost to himself. Then, to her: "You chose this."

Not a threat. A recognition.

He reached into the inner pocket of his coat and withdrew a single photograph. Not one of the ones she'd found. This one was newer. The paper different. He placed it on the table between them.

It felt cold beneath her fingertips. Damp. Wrong.

Lily looked at it. For a split-second, her mind resisted — searching for something else to make of it. A coincidence. A mistake. A stranger's photograph taken at the wrong angle. The thought didn't hold. It couldn't.

The street was hers. The frame captured it from above — from a window that shouldn't have mattered. In the center, blurred by rain and motion, a figure moved toward the building.

Her.

Carrying a suitcase. The date, scrawled in the corner, marked the day she arrived. A week ago. Earlier than it should have been possible.

Her fingers stilled against the edge of the photograph.

"Taken isn't death," Lily said quietly, repeating the word as if testing its shape.

Zane tilted his head.

"You're still thinking in terms of endings," he said. "That's not what this is."

A pause. Deliberate. Measured.

"Nothing ended for them."

The words settled with weight. "They were removed," he continued, his tone shifting — not explaining, but revealing. "When curiosity outweighed caution."

His gaze held hers.

"You were observed," he said. "Patterns. Repetition. Preference."

A beat.

"You're very consistent, Lily."

The sound of the café receded into something distant and indistinct. The world narrowed to the photograph beneath her hand and the precision of his voice.

"Why?" she asked. Steady. Thin. Drawn tight.

Zane didn't answer.

"You think this is a story about me," he said instead. "Or about him."

A pause.

The Collector.

The name didn't echo. It settled.

"It's not," he continued. "It's a story about choices." His gaze didn't waver. "And you just made one."

He stood. The motion was slow. Final.

"The next time you see me," he said, "it won't be in a café."

A breath.

"And the choice won't be yours."

He turned and walked toward the door. Lily didn't watch him leave. Her eyes remained fixed on the photograph — on the version of herself that had already stepped into something she hadn't understood.

She didn't remember standing. Only that, at some point, she had crossed it.

The line.

The mystery was no longer abstract. It was real. It had her face in it. She was already part of it.

And there was no path back.
